## Project snapshot (from submissions)

TrustCommerce is an escrow-based social e-commerce and payment platform: sellers list products/services/contracts on personal storefronts; buyers pay into escrow (not to the seller); funds release on confirmation or after a review-team dispute process. It also aims to act as a multi-rail payment gateway (bKash, Nagad, Rocket, Upay, cards, bank) and later as Escrow-as-a-Service API. Revenue: 2–5% transaction fees, withdrawal fees, premium seller subscriptions, priority dispute fees, and API licensing. Business model submitted as a canvas image; project report is a concise 4-page write-up.

## 1. Innovation & Originality — 3/5

### What was evaluated
Whether escrow + social storefront + dispute resolution is a distinctive invention versus known marketplace/payment patterns (including overlap with escrow ideas already present in this contest, e.g. SebaAI’s escrow framing).

### Analysis
The problem framing—two-sided fraud fear in Facebook/Instagram commerce and informal deals—is accurate and locally grounded. The proposed mechanism (hold funds → confirm → release / dispute) is the classic escrow pattern used by many global and regional platforms. Bundling **personal storefront + multi-MFS payouts + dispute desk + future Escrow-as-a-Service API** is a coherent product package, but it is incremental packaging rather than a new technical or market architecture.

Relative to this cohort: stronger conceptual clarity than a vague “e-commerce app,” weaker originality than Dishari’s tactile navigation, TrueDay’s work simulation, Bidhi’s flat-rate legal market design, or CryptoSilicon’s PUF approach. Supporting informal agreements/bets as use cases is a small differentiator, not a category shift.

### Why this score
**3/5** — Solid, well-articulated applied product in a known escrow/marketplace category; useful local packaging, not high novelty.

---

## 2. Feasibility — 3/5

### What was evaluated
Whether a student team can ship a trustworthy escrow + payments product given regulatory, operational, and partnership constraints described in their own risk section.

### Analysis
**Supporting feasibility:** The report is clear and phased (manual disputes first → MFS rails → reputation/automation → API). Unit economics are stated simply (2–5% take rate + withdrawal/premium fees). Canvas identifies payment partners and dispute ops as core activities. Scope of a Phase-1 marketplace with manual review is conceptually buildable as software.

**Limiting feasibility:** Holding customer funds in Bangladesh requires serious **regulatory/licensing** and partnership with licensed aggregators—called out by the team, correctly, as a major risk. Dispute operations do not scale without headcount or strong automation; collusion and fake disputes need fraud systems they only sketch. Multi-rail MFS + card/bank integration is partnership-heavy. Materials are thinner than peers with live demos or detailed prototypes (4-page report + canvas image; no live URL cited in the extract). Feasibility is medium: direction is right; trust/fintech execution bar is high.

### Why this score
**3/5** — Clear MVP path on paper; payments regulation, escrow custody, and dispute ops keep it from a higher mark.

---

## 3. Impact & Relevance — 4/5

### What was evaluated
How important the trust gap is for Bangladesh online commerce and who would benefit if the platform worked.

### Analysis
Social-commerce fraud and non-payment fear are everyday barriers for Facebook/Instagram sellers, freelancers, and P2P buyers in Bangladesh. Recovering buyers who stopped shopping online and sellers who lose sales to distrust—as the report argues—would expand economic activity in a large informal digital market. That is high **relevance**.

Impact is economic trust and market participation rather than life-critical outcomes (unlike emergency, assistive, or post-harvest cold storage). Still, for a large population of small online sellers and buyers, the problem severity is real and widespread. Escrow-as-a-Service, if achieved, could multiply impact beyond one marketplace.

### Why this score
**4/5** — Strong, widespread BD digital-commerce relevance; not top-tier life/livelihood crisis impact, but clearly above convenience-only campus apps.
